# Gravitational Wave Discoveries and Multi-Messenger Astronomy

## GW150914 — The First Detection

Date: September 14, 2015 (observed 09:50:45 UTC)
Published: Physical Review Letters, February 11, 2016

Source: Binary Black Hole (BBH) merger
- Primary BH mass: ~36 M☉
- Secondary BH mass: ~29 M☉
- Final BH mass: ~62 M☉ (energy radiated: ~3 M☉ c² ~ 5 × 10⁴⁷ J)
- Distance: ~410 Mpc (~1.3 billion light-years)
- Sky localization: ~600 deg² (two LIGO detectors only)

Signal characteristics:
- Duration in band: ~0.2 seconds
- Frequency sweep: ~35 Hz to ~150 Hz
- Peak strain: h ~ 10⁻²¹
- Network SNR: ~24

Significance: false alarm rate < 1 per 200,000 years.

Confirmed: black holes are real astrophysical objects.
Confirmed: binary black holes exist and merge within Hubble time.
Confirmed: gravitational waves exist and travel at the speed of light.
Nobel Prize 2017: Weiss, Barish, Thorne (LIGO founders).

## GW151226 — Boxing Day Event

Date: December 26, 2015
Source: Binary Black Hole merger
Masses: ~14 M☉ + ~8 M☉
Signal: longer in band (~1 second), lower mass → higher frequency at merger.
Network SNR: ~13.

## GW170817 — Binary Neutron Star Merger (Multi-messenger Astronomy)

Date: August 17, 2017
Source: Binary Neutron Star (BNS) merger — first detected!
- NS masses: ~1.17 M☉ and ~1.36 M☉
- Distance: ~40 Mpc (~130 million light-years) — much closer than BBH events
- Sky localization: ~28 deg² (LIGO + Virgo three-detector network)

GW signal:
- Duration: ~100 seconds in band! (much longer than BBH — lower chirp mass)
- Frequency sweep: 24 Hz to ~2000 Hz (merger)
- Peak strain: h ~ 10⁻²²

Electromagnetic counterparts detected:
- GRB 170817A: short gamma-ray burst detected by Fermi-GBM, 1.74 seconds after merger
- AT2017gfo: optical kilonova transient in galaxy NGC 4993
- X-ray, radio afterglow observed weeks later

Multi-messenger science:
- Confirmed: short gamma-ray bursts from BNS mergers
- Confirmed: heavy elements (r-process nucleosynthesis) produced in mergers
  - Gold, platinum, uranium — detected in kilonova spectra!
- Hubble constant measurement: H₀ = 70⁺¹²⁻⁸ km/s/Mpc (independent "standard siren" method)
- Neutron star equation of state constraints
- Speed of gravity = speed of light to 1 part in 10¹⁵

## GWTC Catalogs

GWTC-1 (O1+O2 runs): 11 events (10 BBH, 1 BNS)
GWTC-2 (O3a): 47 events total (including 36 new)
GWTC-2.1 (O3a reanalysis): 90 total candidates
GWTC-3 (O3b): 90 confident GW events total

Event classes observed:
- Binary Black Hole (BBH): most common, ~85% of events
- Binary Neutron Star (BNS): GW170817, GW190425
- Neutron Star-Black Hole (NSBH): GW200105, GW200115
- Mass gap events: GW190521 (intermediate mass black holes, ~66+85 M☉)

## Notable Individual Events

### GW190521
Masses: ~85 M☉ + ~66 M☉ → remnant ~142 M☉ intermediate mass black hole!
Signal duration: <1 second in band.
First confirmed intermediate mass black hole.
Secondary in "upper mass gap" (50-130 M☉ forbidden by pair-instability supernovae).

### GW190814
Masses: ~23 M☉ + 2.6 M☉ — secondary is either most massive neutron star or lightest black hole ever!
Mass ratio: 1:9 (highly asymmetric)
"Mass gap" object: 2.5-5 M☉ (no confirmed BH or NS in this range previously).

### GW190425
Binary Neutron Star merger.
Total mass: ~3.4 M☉ — much more massive than Milky Way BNS population.
No EM counterpart detected (too far, ~160 Mpc).

## Future Detectors and Sources

### Einstein Telescope (ET)
Underground triangle design, arm length: 10 km.
Cryogenic operation (10 K) to reduce thermal noise.
Ten times more sensitive than Advanced LIGO.
Expected: thousands of BNS per year, BH mergers to cosmological distances.
Start: ~2035.

### Cosmic Explorer (CE)
L-shaped, arm length: 20-40 km.
USA next-generation detector.
Sensitivity: ~10× Advanced LIGO.
Could detect BNS out to z > 2 (most of observable universe).

### LISA
Heliocentric orbit, 2.5 million km arms.
Frequency band: 0.1 mHz to 1 Hz.
Sources: supermassive BH mergers, galactic double white dwarf binaries, extreme mass ratio inspirals (EMRIs).
Launch: ~2035.

### Pulsar Timing Arrays (PTAs)
Sensitive to nHz gravitational waves.
NANOGrav 2023: evidence for stochastic GW background!
Sources: supermassive BH binaries in galaxy mergers.
Frequency: ~10 nHz = 1/(several years).

## Multi-Messenger Astronomy

GW + EM: "hearing" and "seeing" the same event.
GW + neutrinos: future possibility (supernova detection).
GW + cosmic rays: indirect correlation studies.

GW170817 opened the era of multi-messenger GW astronomy.
Future BNS events with ET+CE: sub-degree sky localization → easier EM follow-up.
Standard sirens: GW distance + EM redshift → independent H₀ measurement.
With ~50 BNS events: H₀ to ~2% precision → resolve Hubble tension!
