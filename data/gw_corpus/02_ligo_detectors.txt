# LIGO, Virgo, and Gravitational Wave Detectors

## Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory (LIGO)

LIGO consists of two L-shaped detectors in the USA:
- LIGO Hanford (H1): Hanford, Washington State
- LIGO Livingston (L1): Livingston, Louisiana
- Separation: ~3,000 km (10 ms light travel time)

Each detector has two perpendicular arms, each 4 km long.
Arm tubes are evacuated to pressures of ~10⁻⁹ torr (ultra-high vacuum).

## Interferometry Principle

LIGO uses Michelson interferometry to detect tiny changes in arm length.
A laser beam is split at a beamsplitter; each beam travels down one arm.
Mirrors at the ends reflect the beams back to the beamsplitter.
The beams recombine and interfere — the pattern reveals any difference in arm length.

When a GW passes, one arm is stretched while the other is compressed.
This creates a differential arm length change: ΔL = h × L
For h = 10⁻²¹ and L = 4 km: ΔL ~ 4 × 10⁻¹⁸ m

Power recycling cavity: boosts input laser power from ~150W to ~100 kW in the arms.
Signal recycling cavity: optimizes response at GW frequencies.

## Fabry-Pérot Cavities

The arms are Fabry-Pérot resonant cavities (not simple mirrors).
Finesse F ~ 450 means light bounces ~300 times before exiting.
Effective arm length: L_eff = F/π × L ~ 1200 km
This dramatically increases the phase shift from a given GW strain.

Storage time of light in cavity: τ ≈ 2FL/(πc) ~ 2.7 ms
This limits sensitivity above ~300 Hz.

## Advanced LIGO (aLIGO)

Advanced LIGO (upgraded 2015) uses quantum optics techniques:
- 40 kg fused silica test masses (mirrors)
- High laser power (~200W input, ~750 kW in Fabry-Pérot cavities)
- Seismic isolation: active and passive multi-stage isolation systems
- Thermal noise mitigation: monolithic mirror suspensions, cryogenic cooling planned

Sensitivity: strain noise floor ~ 10⁻²³ /√Hz at best frequency (~150-300 Hz)
Frequency range: ~10 Hz to ~5000 Hz (sensitive band: ~20-2000 Hz)

## Noise Sources in LIGO

### Seismic Noise (low frequency, <20 Hz)
Ground vibrations from earthquakes, ocean waves ("microseismic hump"), human activity.
Couples into detector through suspensions.
Mitigated by: multi-stage pendulum suspensions, active seismic isolation (HEPI, HAM-ISI).
Sets low-frequency wall: LIGO not sensitive below ~10-20 Hz.

### Thermal Noise (10-200 Hz)
Random thermal fluctuations of atoms in mirror coatings and substrates.
Fluctuation-dissipation theorem: S_x(f) = (4k_B T / ω) Im[χ(ω)]
Types:
- Mirror coating thermal noise (dominant ~50-200 Hz)
- Suspension fiber thermal noise (~10-50 Hz)
- Substrate (bulk) Brownian noise
Mitigated by: very low-loss materials (fused silica), low-noise coatings (Ta₂O₅/SiO₂).

### Shot Noise (high frequency, >200 Hz)
Quantum noise from photon counting statistics.
S_shot ∝ 1/P_laser (decreases with more laser power)
Standard quantum limit: Δx_SQL = √(ℏ/mω²τ)
Mitigated by: squeezed light injection (reduces photon number uncertainty below shot noise).

### Quantum Radiation Pressure Noise (low frequency)
Higher laser power increases quantum back-action from photon momentum kicks.
S_QRPN ∝ P_laser
There is a trade-off: more power reduces shot noise but increases radiation pressure noise.
Standard Quantum Limit (SQL) sets the minimum of combined quantum noise.

### Quantum Noise Below the SQL (Squeezing)
Frequency-dependent squeezing allows beating the SQL.
First demonstrated at LIGO 2019.
Injects squeezed vacuum states to reduce noise in measured quadrature.

### Non-Gaussian Noise / Glitches
Transient noise artifacts: "glitches" from various instrumental/environmental sources.
Types: scattered light, thermally-induced mirror jerks, electronics noise spikes.
Glitch rates: several per hour in initial LIGO, ~1/day in O3.
Detection challenge: short-duration glitches overlap with GW signals.
Classification: used ML (Gravity Spy, GravitySpy CNN) to identify glitch types.

## Other Detectors

### Virgo (Italy)
Located near Pisa, Italy. Arm length: 3 km.
Part of LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA network.
Critical for sky localization of GW sources (triangulation).
Detected GW170817 jointly with LIGO.

### KAGRA (Japan)
Underground (Kamioka mine) cryogenic detector.
Arm length: 3 km. Mirror temperature: 20 K (cryogenic).
Underground location greatly reduces seismic noise.

### LISA (Space-based, future ~2035)
Laser Interferometer Space Antenna.
Three spacecraft in heliocentric orbit, arm length: 2.5 million km.
Targets: supermassive BH mergers, galactic binaries, extreme mass ratio inspirals.
Frequency band: 0.1 mHz to 1 Hz (complementary to ground-based).

## Detector Characterization

Noise PSD S_n(f): measured from detector output when no signal present.
Amplitude spectral density (ASD): √S_n(f) in units of strain/√Hz.
Typical LIGO ASD: ~10⁻²³ strain/√Hz at 150 Hz.

Calibration: convert photodetector readout (Watts) to strain (dimensionless).
Uncertainty: ~1% amplitude, ~10 ms phase.

Data quality flags: mark times with known noise issues (PEM sensors, control systems).
Categories: CAT1 (detector not operating), CAT2 (non-astrophysical transients), CAT3.

## Network Operations

Single detector: can't distinguish GW from seismic glitch.
Two detectors (LIGO H+L): require signal in both within 10 ms light travel time.
Three+ detectors: triangulate sky position using time delays.
Sky localization improves with more detectors: ~100s deg² with 2 detectors, ~few deg² with 3+.

Coincidence search: find events in multiple detectors within allowed time window.
Coherent search: combine data from all detectors with appropriate phase relationships.
