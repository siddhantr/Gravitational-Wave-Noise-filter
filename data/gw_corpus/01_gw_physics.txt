# Gravitational Waves — Physics and Theory

## What Are Gravitational Waves?

Gravitational waves are ripples in the fabric of spacetime caused by the acceleration of massive objects.
They were predicted by Albert Einstein in 1916 as a consequence of his General Theory of Relativity.
The first direct detection was made on September 14, 2015 by the LIGO detectors (GW150914).

Gravitational waves travel at the speed of light: c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s.
They carry energy and angular momentum away from their source.
As gravitational waves pass, they alternately stretch and squeeze spacetime in directions perpendicular to propagation.

## General Relativity Basics

Einstein's field equations: G_μν = (8πG/c⁴) T_μν
G_μν = Einstein tensor (spacetime curvature)
T_μν = stress-energy tensor (matter and energy content)
G = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ m³ kg⁻¹ s⁻² (gravitational constant)

The metric tensor g_μν describes the geometry of spacetime.
Flat (Minkowski) spacetime: ds² = -c²dt² + dx² + dy² + dz²
In the weak-field limit: g_μν = η_μν + h_μν where |h_μν| << 1

The linearized Einstein equations yield a wave equation for h_μν:
□h̄_μν = -16πG/c⁴ T_μν
where □ is the d'Alembertian operator and h̄_μν is the trace-reversed perturbation.

## Gravitational Wave Polarizations

Gravitational waves have two independent polarization states:
- Plus polarization (h₊): stretches in x, squeezes in y (and vice versa)
- Cross polarization (h×): stretches/squeezes at 45° to plus polarization

The strain h measures the fractional change in distance:
h = ΔL / L
where ΔL is the change in arm length and L is the original arm length.

Typical strain amplitudes from astrophysical sources: h ~ 10⁻²¹ to 10⁻²³
This means for LIGO (arm length L = 4 km): ΔL ~ 10⁻¹⁸ m (1/1000 the size of a proton!)

## Gravitational Wave Sources

### Compact Binary Coalescence (CBC)
Binary systems of two compact objects (black holes, neutron stars, white dwarfs) spiraling inward.
As they orbit, they radiate gravitational wave energy, causing the orbit to shrink (inspiral).
Eventually they merge, producing a final "ringdown" as the merged object settles.

Phases of CBC:
1. Inspiral: slow spiral, chirp signal increasing in frequency and amplitude
2. Merger: peak strain, highly nonlinear dynamics
3. Ringdown: quasi-normal modes of the merged black hole/neutron star

### Continuous Gravitational Waves
From spinning asymmetric neutron stars (pulsars with "mountains").
Nearly monochromatic (single frequency) signals.
Very weak amplitude, require long observation times.

### Burst Sources
Supernovae, cosmic string interactions, unknown sources.
Short-duration, broadband signals.

### Stochastic Background
Superposition of many unresolved sources.
Cosmological background from inflation or early universe phase transitions.

## Chirp Signal

A chirp is the gravitational waveform from a binary inspiral.
Frequency increases (sweeps up) as the binary loses energy to gravitational radiation.

Chirp mass (M_c): determines the evolution of the orbital frequency.
M_c = (m₁ m₂)^(3/5) / (m₁ + m₂)^(1/5)

Orbital frequency evolution: df/dt = (96/5) π^(8/3) (G M_c/c³)^(5/3) f^(11/3)

GW frequency (twice orbital frequency): f_GW = 2 f_orbital

At merger: f_GW ~ c³/(6^(3/2) π G M_total)
For 30+30 solar mass black holes: f_merger ~ 150 Hz

Strain amplitude of inspiral:
h(t) = (4/r) (G M_c/c²) (π G M_c f/c³)^(2/3)
where r = distance to source.

## The Quadrupole Formula

Power radiated in gravitational waves (quadrupole approximation):
P = -(G/5c⁵) <d³Iij/dt³ d³Iij/dt³>
where Iij is the mass quadrupole moment tensor.

This is the famous quadrupole formula, valid for slow-moving, weak-field sources.
A sphere has zero quadrupole radiation — only asymmetric motions radiate.
Binary orbit produces quadrupole radiation because the mass distribution changes periodically.

## Energy and Frequency Evolution

Energy of Newtonian circular orbit: E = -G m₁ m₂ / (2a) where a = semi-major axis
Energy radiated per orbit: ΔE = (32/5) G⁴ m₁² m₂² (m₁+m₂) / (c⁵ a⁵) · 2π/ω

Orbital decay: da/dt = -(64/5) G³ m₁ m₂ (m₁+m₂) / (c⁵ a³)

Peters formula for merger timescale:
T_merge = (12/19) a₀⁴ c₀ / (β G(m₁+m₂)) × integral term
where a₀ = initial separation.

## Strain Sensitivity and Detection

Signal-to-noise ratio: SNR = sqrt(4 ∫|h̃(f)|²/S_n(f) df)
h̃(f) = Fourier transform of strain signal h(t)
S_n(f) = one-sided noise power spectral density [1/Hz]

Optimal matched filter: cross-correlate signal with template waveform.
SNR > 8 typically required for confident detection.
Network of detectors: SNR² = Σ SNR²_detector (for coherent combination).

Horizon distance: the maximum distance at which a source is detectable (SNR ≥ 8).
LIGO's binary neutron star horizon: ~200 Mpc
LIGO's binary black hole (30+30 M☉) horizon: ~5 Gpc
